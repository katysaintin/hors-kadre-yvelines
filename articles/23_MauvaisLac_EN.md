# The Wrong Lake

*You didn't fail. The system just couldn't read you.*

*Medium — Katy Saintin | May 2026*

---

> *"Everybody is a genius. But if you judge a fish by its ability
> to climb a tree, it will live its whole life believing
> that it is stupid."*
>
> Widely attributed to Einstein. Most likely written by Matthew Kelly
> in 2004. The author is wrong. The idea is right.

This quote is everywhere. On school walls. In graduation speeches.
On TED Talk slides.

And then, the following Monday, students are ranked by their grades.

**We quote the fish. We never change the lake.**

But this article asks a question the fish story doesn't:

**What if the fish was already in a lake — just the wrong one?**

---

## What I lived

I spent seven years in one of France's most demanding boarding
schools. Science track. I worked relentlessly.

I graduated without honors. And convinced I was average.

Not stupid — I'd understood that much. Just... insufficient.
Too dreamy. A student who couldn't tick the right boxes
despite every effort.

The following year, I enrolled in a two-year technology institute
— applied engineering. Four girls out of a hundred.
Exactly the kind of place people advised me against,
given "my level."

Something unlocked. Not because of an exceptional teacher.
Just — applied mathematics, computer science, concrete projects.
An environment where intelligence no longer looked like what
I'd spent seven years learning to imitate.

I graduated top of my class.

**I was born at 37.**

That was the year of my cognitive assessment — taken after
my son's. What I discovered didn't change my brain.
It made me legible to myself.

ADHD diagnosis at 46. Not a birth — an inventory.
I realized that everything I had built, I had built
with an undetected disability. Not despite myself.
With myself. As I am.

**That wasn't resilience. That was recalibration.**

*(Spoiler: I now run the control system of ISEULT —
one of the most powerful MRI machines in the world.
But we're not there yet. 😄)*

## What the diagnosis actually taught me — about other people

Many people, after a late diagnosis, feel a legitimate anger.
At the school that didn't see. The doctors who didn't look.
The colleagues who didn't follow.

I understand that anger. It makes sense.

Mine went the other way.

I finally understood why others couldn't keep up with me.
Not because they were obtuse or indifferent —
because they were in their own lake too.
A neurotypical lake, built for neurotypical brains.
They were swimming just fine in their environment.
I was the one in the wrong lake.

**That distinction changed everything.**

I stopped being frustrated. I started understanding.
And something unexpected happened: I developed genuine empathy
— not resignation, real empathy —
for people whose wiring had felt foreign to me my whole life.

That's when I discovered NVC — Nonviolent Communication.
Not as a conflict management tool.
As a common language between brains that don't naturally
speak the same dialect.

And my sensitivity — which I had spent decades experiencing
as a flaw, something to hide from other people's judgement —
I finally accepted it for what it was.
A way of being in the world. Not a manufacturing defect.

I didn't get fixed. I got recalibrated.

---

And then I went looking for my people.
What I found surprised me.

In the communities supposedly built for people like me...

I found the same mechanism all over again.

ADHD groups: *"You're professionally successful?
Then you're not really ADHD."*

Gifted groups: *"Did your kids skip grades?
What special activities do they do?"*

No. My children — 147 and 145 IQ on the WISC —
are perfectly happy in their regular classes, with their peers.
No grade-skipping required.
A high IQ opens possibilities. It doesn't mandate a script.

I ended up feeling more at ease with my Asperger husband
and my neurotypical friends than with communities
that were supposed to understand me.

Not out of disappointment. Out of clarity.

Because I stopped looking for a lake that looked like me.
I started looking for people who actually see me.

**Those are not the same thing.**

## What the environment actually does to the brain

Here is the question the system never asks:

**It's not "can this fish swim?"**
**It's "does this lake allow it to?"**

This is not a motivational metaphor. It's neurobiology.

Brain plasticity — documented since the 1990s, confirmed
by hundreds of studies — says this: the brain is not a fixed
profile set at birth. It develops, reconfigures, adapts
continuously based on what its environment offers.

**The same brain can produce radically different outcomes
depending on the lake it evolves in.**

### The Gennevilliers experiment

In 2011, Céline Alvarez — a teacher in a disadvantaged school
in Gennevilliers, outside Paris — launched an experiment
in her kindergarten class. Method inspired by Maria Montessori,
enriched by cognitive neuroscience. Prepared environment,
freedom of movement, hands-on learning, peer cooperation.

Results after one year, documented by a research team from
the École Normale Supérieure:
- Children were reading at age 5
- Their cognitive scores exceeded the national average
- Including children who had started with significant
  difficulties

These children hadn't changed. **The lake had changed.**

The French Ministry of Education closed her experimental
class in 2014. Officially for administrative reasons.

We never change the lake. Even when the proof is right there.

### From inside a machine that explores the brain

I don't talk about neuroscience from a book.

I run the control system of ISEULT — the 11.7 Tesla MRI
developed at CEA-Neurospin. A scientific instrument that
explores the human brain at unprecedented resolution.

What researchers observe there confirms what Céline Alvarez
showed in a disadvantaged school classroom:

**What shapes a brain is not the box you put it in.
It's the richness of what you give it to explore.**

## The wrong lake isn't always school

This mechanism doesn't stop at 18.

In the workplace, the same profile will be seen as disorganised
in an overly rigid environment — and perfectly effective
in a context that gives them space and real problems to solve.

In relationships, the same brain will be exhausting where
conformity is demanded — and energising where its way
of connecting things is valued.

**The wrong lake is any environment designed for a different
brain than yours — that makes you believe the problem is you.**

---

## Boxes within boxes

There is something uncomfortable worth naming.

The neurodiversity movement — the one defending exactly
what I just described — sometimes reproduces the same mechanism.

ADHD. ASD. Gifted. Dyslexic. Dyspraxic.
"Twice exceptional." "Zebra profile."

Finer boxes. But still boxes.

And when you don't fit into any of them — or fit into
several at once — you don't exist any more than before.

Gifted + ADHD + woman + professional success?

*"That's not possible. You're just burned out."*

The gifted profile in its standard box: the brilliant student
who skips grades. Not the one who has been quietly
compensating for 40 years with a very high verbal IQ
and a deficient working memory.

The ADHD profile in its standard box: the hyperactive boy
who can't sit still. Not the woman daydreaming her way
through meetings while delivering complex projects —
and losing her keys every morning.

**The lake has a new name. The mechanism is identical.**

The right lake isn't a custom-built lake for each fish.
It's a lake rich enough for very different profiles to thrive
— without needing to know in advance who is what.

## To close

We don't reveal potential. We create the conditions
for it to appear.

Some people discover this at 20. Others at 37.
Others at 46. Others never — because no one ever questioned
the system's verdict.

**The lake was there all along.**
**No one told me I could swim.**
**So I learned to believe I couldn't.**
