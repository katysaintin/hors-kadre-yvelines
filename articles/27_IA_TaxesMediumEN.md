# The System That Speaks a Language Nobody Taught You

*How AI became the first thing that actually explained what I was supposed to fill in.*

*By Katy Ho | Hors Kadre*

Every year, it's the same ritual.

I open my tax filing platform. I stare at the boxes. The boxes stare back.

Box 1099-DIV. Schedule C. Line 17b.

*I break out in hives just thinking about it.* 😅

I'm a software engineer. I debug complex industrial control systems in real time. I read software architecture diagrams before my morning coffee. I've given talks at international conferences in New York, Oxford, and Johannesburg.

And taxes? Three weeks. Minimum. With Sunday-night dread every single day of the week.

Not because I'm stupid.

Because the system speaks a language nobody actually teaches you.


## The Language Nobody Speaks

Here's a concrete example. I own a rental property. In my property management statements, the charges are called *maintenance fees*. In the tax form, the corresponding box asks for *provisions for deductible charges*.

Same thing. Two different words. No visible link.

I'm an engineer, not a mind reader.

Before we go further: you're not a special case.

So if you've ever closed the tax portal saying "I'll deal with this tomorrow" — and tomorrow became mid-April — you're statistically in the silent majority.

This is not a personal failure. It's a design failure.


## The Bank Teller of 1997

*(Yes, 1997. I'm too old for this s**t.)*

I was 17. I had some money to move from my current account to a savings account.

The bank teller told me, with the full gravity of her professional training:

> *"You will become liable in full upon entering active employment status."*

Blank stare from me.

*(We're Asian — so the "she's speaking Chinese to me" look had an extra layer of irony. Free comedy, no subscription required.)*

My older sister — no finance degree, no banking background — translated in two seconds:

> *"She means: when you start working, you'll owe your first year of taxes all at once."*

And me, relieved, with my legendary tact, still standing in front of the teller:

> *"Ohhh. But why didn't she just say that?"*

The teller wasn't wrong.

She had simply forgotten that jargon is a barrier, not a badge of expertise.

My sister was doing what linguists call Plain Language — the practice of making written and spoken content clear and accessible to everyone, regardless of education or background. Not reserved for people with disabilities. Not reserved for beginners. Reserved for everyone. Because unnecessary complexity is not a virtue.

AI does Plain Language too.

And apparently, that bothers people.


## What Actually Bothers People

A post making the rounds lately:

> *"AI says incorrect things with the same confidence as when it's right."*

True.

But you know what else does that?

The lawyer who warns against using AI to read contracts — in an article written, footnote included, *with the help of a generative AI tool*.

*(I'm just saying.)*

**Misplaced confidence is not an AI invention. It's a deeply human tradition, and it's free.**

We accept without question: spellcheck, calculators, Google Maps, Wikipedia.

We reject: AI explaining what to put in box 1099-DIV.

Because it's not the tool that bothers people.

**It's what the tool makes possible.**

When knowledge circulates freely, the scarcity of expertise decreases. And that bothers those whose legitimacy depends precisely on that scarcity.


## A Supply Teacher, One Room, One Hour

A few years ago, at a university somewhere in the suburbs of Paris, a Java object-oriented programming catch-up class was held on a weekday evening.

A supply teacher walked in. No formal teaching qualification. Never handed out a blank test sheet with the air of doing someone a favor.

Facing them: students who had spent three months in a programming course. Three months. With a tenured professor — never a day in industry — who started every session with *"take out a sheet of paper, surprise quiz."* When one student asked if he could use colored pencils for syntax highlighting... the answer was not memorable for good reasons.

Result after three months: nothing understood, nothing learned, nothing built.

The supply teacher started with Hello World. Built up gradually. Pizza slices to explain classes. Pikachu for methods. Months had gone into building those courses — without AI, in 2016.

At the end of the session — one hour, just one — a student wrote:

> *"I understood everything about Java in 30 minutes with you. We've had three months of classes and learned nothing. Thank you. That was brilliant."*

Same subject. Same level. Opposite results.

The difference? Not the degree. Not the seniority. Not the jargon.

**The explanation.**

That supply teacher was a woman.

And that woman was me — in 2016, without AI. *(I'm too old for this s**t.)*
