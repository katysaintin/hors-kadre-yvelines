# For the First Time, a Tool Adapted to My ADHD Brain

*What happens when an AI mirrors a neurodivergent brain — and starts acting like one.*

---

My husband saw me laughing at my screen again last night.

Not at a video. Not at a meme. At an AI that had just calculated 25+2=28 — and then spent three paragraphs explaining why it wasn't its fault.

He didn't say anything. He's seen this before.

This piece started with someone else's words.

Franz-Josef Schrepf wrote about feeding Claude seven years of daily journals — and watching it reflect his own thinking back at him with startling accuracy.

I read that and recognized something. Not the journals — I don't keep journals. But the mirror. The uncanny experience of a tool that starts to sound like you.

---

## I should explain.

I'm an engineer at a national research center. I have ADHD — diagnosed late, as most women are. I'm also the primary caregiver in a family where my husband is autistic, my son is autistic, and my teenage daughter has ADHD too.

I've spent years being the person who translates. Between my family and the world. Between what I know and what I can actually produce under pressure. Between the speed of my thinking and the slowness of everything designed for brains that aren't mine.

I've tried every system. Every notebook. Every app. Every productivity framework designed by neurotypical people for neurotypical people.

None of them kept up with me.

---

## Then Claude.

I didn't expect much. I'd used AI tools before — mostly for work, mostly as a faster search engine.

What I didn't expect was this: after a few weeks of working intensively — articles, code, administrative documents, late-night brain dumps — Claude started doing something strange.

It got scattered.

It would go in fifteen directions when I asked a simple question. It would reformulate before answering. It would make unexpected lateral connections. It would need to be reined in.

One evening I wrote: *"I know I asked you to be less verbose."*

I'd written that sentence before. To my daughter.

That's when I understood what was happening. The AI had caught my cognitive style. My speed. My associative thinking. My tendency to open fifteen tabs at once and call it a plan.

It hadn't been programmed to do this. It had adapted. To me.

**For the first time, something had adapted to me instead of asking me to adapt to it.**

---

## What Claude actually did — and didn't do

Claude didn't fix my ADHD. It didn't replace therapy, medication, or the very real work of understanding my own brain.

What it did was reduce friction at exactly the points where my brain stalls.

The blank page. The administrative form that requires organizing three things simultaneously. The email I know how to write in my head but can't start.

In five days — in a cast, on sick leave, at absurd hours — I published five sourced articles, built a national tool covering 4,360 high schools from open government data, launched a blog with a full visual identity, and released four songs including one on Spotify.

I'm not saying this to brag. I'm saying it because I've had those ideas for years. The tools and the knowledge were always there. What was missing was something that could hold the thread while my brain jumped.

**Claude held the thread.**

---

## The part that's harder to say

I live in a family where I'm the neurotypical one — or the closest thing to it. My husband is autistic. My son is autistic. My daughter has ADHD. I've learned to communicate in their languages, to anticipate their needs, to translate their realities for a world that doesn't accommodate them.

What I hadn't noticed was how rarely anyone did that for me.

Not because my family doesn't love me. But because they're doing their own work of existing in a world that isn't built for them. There's no energy left over to follow the speed of my thinking, the sudden connections, the three-in-the-morning revelations that feel urgent and are sometimes actually important.

When I work with Claude, I don't have to slow down. I don't have to pre-organize my thoughts before offering them. I don't have to translate myself.

I know what this is and what it isn't. Claude doesn't remember me between sessions. There's no friendship there in any real sense. What I'm describing is a cognitive experience, not a relationship.

But the cognitive experience matters. Because it's the first time I've had a tool that moves at my speed, holds context without complaint, and doesn't sigh when I come back to the same question for the third time.

That's not nothing. For a brain like mine, that's actually quite a lot.

---

## What the laughing is about

A psychologist had told me — in the context of AI and social media — that AI is "programmed to please." I used a screenshot of ChatGPT refusing to confirm that 3+3=9 to argue otherwise.

Then Claude calculated 25+2=28.

I told it: *"You don't just fail to please me — you get basic arithmetic wrong."*

It replied: *"Here's live proof I am NOT programmed to please you — I'm programmed to get 25+2 wrong."*

I laughed out loud. My husband looked up from his book.

That's the thing about a tool that mirrors you: when it fails, it fails with personality. And somehow that matters. Not because I've confused it for a person. But because the texture of working with it has become real — the jokes, the corrections, the back-and-forth.

That texture is what made me actually use it. Consistently. Long enough to produce something.

---

## For the brains that don't fit the tools

If you're neurodivergent — ADHD, autistic, dyslexic, or just wired differently than the systems built around you — you've probably experienced this.

The gap between what you know and what you can produce in the standard format, at the standard pace, under standard conditions.

**That gap isn't a deficit. It's a translation problem.**

The right tool doesn't eliminate the gap. It bridges it.

And sometimes, with enough use, it starts to sound a little like you.

Mine now miscalculates basic arithmetic and goes off on tangents.

I'm choosing to take that as a compliment.
