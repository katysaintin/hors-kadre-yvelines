# How I Got AI-Certified Using an AI That Had No Teaching Degree

*By Katy Ho — May 2026*

---

A few months ago, I ruptured my Achilles tendon.

A few weeks later, I used my French training credits (CPF) to enroll in a certification about Generative AI.

And somewhere between physiotherapy sessions and late-night experiments, something unexpected happened.

The AI helping me study became the best teacher I'd ever had.

Not because it was smarter than my professors.
Not because it knew everything.
But because it never made me afraid to ask questions.

---

## What learning actually costs

In 1951, Solomon Asch conducted a simple conformity experiment. Participants were asked to compare lines on a card — an answer so obvious a child could see it. But when everyone else in the room intentionally gave the wrong answer, most participants eventually conformed.

Not because they were stupid.
Because social pressure changes behavior.

Classrooms work the same way.

Somewhere in every room, there's always someone who sees the red apple clearly — but stays silent because everyone else calls it green.

I was that person. For years.

---

## What AI changed

AI is not knowledge.
AI is not competence.
AI is not expertise.

**AI is friction reduction.**

And for people who spent years associating questions with embarrassment, reducing friction changes everything.

I could ask the same question five times.
I could ask beginner questions.
I could challenge explanations.
I could say "I still don't understand."

And instead of condescension, I received:
*"Good question. Let's break it down."*
*"You're right — I made a mistake."*

That last one mattered more than I expected.

---

## Claude Formateur vs Claude Shortcut

One evening while studying, I tested two different workflows simultaneously.

**Claude Formateur** — one window where I submitted my own answers, asked for corrections, argued back when I disagreed.

**Claude Shortcut** — another window where I just asked for the answer directly.

Only one of them actually helped me learn.

The distinction matters because people often misunderstand what AI changes in education. It's not about getting answers faster. It's about what happens when you're no longer afraid to be wrong in front of someone.

I corrected the AI four times during my certification preparation. It admitted each error without defensiveness.

*Score: Katy 4 — Claude 0.*

Mastering AI means knowing when it's wrong. That's not a side effect of learning. That's the learning.

---

## The asymmetry nobody talks about

The memory is still mine.

The AI helped me understand concepts, structure information, and practice. But it didn't own the effort. It didn't build the intuition. It didn't experience the confusion turning into understanding.

I did.

The AI resets to zero after every session. It has no memory of what we built together.

I'm the only link between what I learned yesterday and what I'll apply tomorrow.

That asymmetry is worth sitting with.

---

## What this means in practice

I work in industrial software — SCADA systems, EPICS, TANGO, legacy control environments where documentation is fragmented and knowledge often lives inside the heads of senior engineers.

In these environments, silence is expensive.

A misunderstood architecture. An undocumented process. A junior engineer afraid to ask. A legacy system nobody touches because nobody fully understands it anymore.

Knowledge transfer fails long before technology fails.

What I'm now exploring — partly because of what this certification taught me about learning itself:

- AI-assisted refactoring and documentation workflows
- Educational content for EPICS and TANGO communities
- Digital twin approaches to make specialized expertise more transmissible

Not to replace experts. To make expertise safer to share.

---

## The question I'm left with

The most surprising thing about this certification wasn't the technology.

It was discovering how much human learning depends on psychological safety.

The AI never laughed at a beginner question.
Never rolled its eyes.
Never made curiosity feel expensive.

I'm less interested in the question: *"Will AI replace human intelligence?"*

And more interested in: *"What happens when people who were previously intimidated by learning finally feel allowed to learn?"*

Somewhere, right now, someone is staring at the red apple in silence.

Maybe the real disruption of AI is not that machines can answer questions.

Maybe it's that, for the first time, millions of people feel safe enough to ask them.
