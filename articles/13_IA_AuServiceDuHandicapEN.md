# AI didn't make my life easier — it made it possible

## For some brains, AI isn't about productivity. It's the difference between functioning and not.


I used to spend hours staring at a blank page.

Not because I had nothing to say.
But because everything was there — all at once.

In 2026, most conversations about AI revolve around one question:

Is it dangerous?

Will it make us dependent? Will it replace our thinking? Will it make us lazy?

These concerns are legitimate. But they all assume the same starting point:

👉 a brain that works "normally"

A brain that can plan easily, structure ideas, start tasks without friction, write on demand, and stay focused under pressure.

Not everyone has that.


## The part no one talks about

We talk about what AI might take away.
We rarely talk about what it already gives back.

For years, I was told to adapt. Work harder. Stay organized. Stop overthinking.

Not because I lacked intelligence.
Because my brain doesn't operate the way the system expects.

I am a SCADA engineer at CEA-Irfu, a PhD candidate combining AI and industrial control systems, a teacher in a software engineering school, and a parent representative.

I was also diagnosed with ADHD late — as most women are.

For years, the problem wasn't thinking.
It was holding everything together long enough to act.


## The numbers behind the invisible

This is not a rare situation.

In France alone, an estimated 2 million adults live with ADHD.
Only 1% have an official diagnosis (Song et al., The Lancet Psychiatry, 2021).

Across all disability types, more than 80% of disabilities in France are invisible — meaning they leave no external trace, require no wheelchair, and trigger no immediate recognition (INSEE).

What the neuroscience shows:

89% of adults with ADHD present deficits in executive functions — the cognitive system responsible for planning, starting tasks, and managing time (Barkley, 2024).

Working memory — the ability to hold multiple pieces of information while acting on them — is reduced by 30 to 40% compared to the general population (Rapport & Alderson, 2008).

This is not a character flaw.
This is physiology.


## The invisible barrier

Imagine this:

You understand everything.
You know exactly what needs to be done.

But when you face a blank page, your brain freezes.

Not from lack of ideas.
From overload.

For developers in the room: imagine your brain manages a LIFO queue instead of a FIFO. The last task entered is processed first — regardless of priority. Or worse: nothing exits at all. The brain processes, analyses, loops — and produces nothing because it cannot find a starting point.

This is not laziness.
This is what neuroscience calls executive dysfunction.


## Then came AI

Not as a shortcut.
Not as a replacement for thinking.

But as a structure.

A way to organize thoughts, break complexity into steps, and externalize what was stuck inside.

The three principles that make AI work for ADHD brains — documented by Safren et al. (2023):

**1. Cognitive externalization** — offloading working memory deficits
**2. Temporal structuring** — compensating for altered time perception
**3. Friction reduction** — eliminating the starting block

AI conversational tools are the first mass-market technology that enables all three simultaneously, in natural language, without technical setup.


## What changed — in concrete terms

During a period where I was physically immobilized — five days, a leg in a cast — I wrote ten articles, built a national interactive tool covering 4,360 French high schools using official open data, launched a blog, and composed four songs including one now on Spotify.

Not because I suddenly became more disciplined.
But because I was using AI.

The tool didn't replace my thinking.
It finally kept up with it.

Before AI:

👉 hours staring at a blank page
👉 switching between 47 open tabs
👉 knowing exactly what to say — unable to start

With AI:

👉 brain dump in natural language
👉 structure emerges from the chaos
👉 execution follows thought without the friction tax


## This is not about comfort

This is about access.

For some people, AI is not a convenience.
It is a cognitive prosthesis.

A tool that adapts to intention instead of forcing the brain to adapt to the tool.

Like a hearing aid that doesn't replace hearing — it restores access.
Like a wheelchair that doesn't replace legs — it restores mobility.

Using AI to compensate for executive dysfunction is not cheating.
It is equalization.


## The real misunderstanding

We talk about dependency, danger, automation.

But we ignore something fundamental:

not all brains start from the same place.

Some people don't need more discipline.
They need better tools.

And here is the calculation that no employer seems to make:

A Claude Pro subscription costs around €20 per month.
A long-term burnout leave costs between €50,000 and €150,000 in lost productivity, recruitment, and training (HR research, 2024).

The question is not whether AI is worth the investment.
The question is why we are still asking it.


## What you call dependency…

Some call finally breathing.

When Jason Winter — a tech entrepreneur with ADHD — read these words on LinkedIn, he wrote:

*"Building my AI workflow over the last year has helped me to stop apologizing for how I work. Not needing to mask isn't a small thing. It's the difference between spending the day performing and spending the day actually making something."*

This is not an isolated experience.
This is what happens when the right tool finally meets the right brain.


## Who are we leaving behind without it?

65% of people with disabilities see technology as an opportunity for employment.
But only 28% see AI specifically as an opportunity — compared to 38% of the general public (Agefiph/Ifop survey, 2023).

Distrust is highest among the people most concerned.

Because no one has explained the difference between the AI that watches them — and the AI they can use.


## Final thought

AI does not replace intelligence.

It gives it a place to exist.

The real question is not whether AI is dangerous.

It is who we are designing the future for —
and who we are leaving out of the conversation.
