# We Are Afraid of the Wrong Tool

## AI is not the problem. The attention economy is.

I keep seeing the same fear everywhere.

"AI is dangerous."
"It will make us dependent."
"It will replace our thinking."

Maybe.

But what if we are simply afraid of the wrong tool?

## A confusion that changes everything

When people talk about AI, they often mix together two completely different systems:

- AI tools designed to help you think
- Platforms designed to capture your attention

These are not the same thing.

One is built to **reduce cognitive friction**.
The other is built to **maximize it — just in the wrong way.**

And yet, in public discussions, they are constantly confused.

## The real machine shaping our behavior

If you want to understand dependency, distraction, and loss of focus, you don't have to look at AI.

You already have the perfect example in your pocket.

Social media platforms are designed around one core principle:

👉 capture and keep your attention for as long as possible

This is not speculation.

Sean Parker, former president of Facebook, explained it clearly:

> "We need to give you a little dopamine hit every once in a while."

Internal Facebook documents, later revealed by whistleblower Frances Haugen in 2021, confirmed this was a deliberate design choice — not a side effect.

For several years, Facebook's algorithm amplified emotional reactions — with "angry" reactions weighted up to five times more than likes.

These systems are not neutral.
They are optimized for engagement — not for thinking.

## The scale of the problem

This is not marginal.

According to Médiamétrie (2024), the average user spends more than **2 hours per day** on social media.

Research published in *NeuroImage* (2021) shows that even short exposure — around 15 minutes — can activate the brain's dopaminergic system.

The result is a feedback loop:

👉 more stimulation
👉 more fragmentation
👉 less sustained attention

In 2024, Oxford University Press chose **"brain rot"** as its word of the year.

Not because of AI.
Because of what constant low-quality digital consumption is doing to our minds.

## And yet, the tool we actually fear is not the one causing this

It's the one trying to fix it.

## A personal shift

I'm an engineer. My work is structured, complex, system-based.

I was also diagnosed with ADHD late — as most women are.

For years, the problem wasn't thinking.
It was holding everything together long enough to act.

Then something changed.

During a period where I was physically immobilized — five days, a leg in a cast — I wrote ten articles and built a structured dataset covering 4,360 high schools across France, using official open data from the Ministry of Education.

Not because I suddenly became more disciplined.
But because I was using AI.

**The tool didn't replace my thinking.**
**It finally kept up with it.**

## AI works differently

AI doesn't need your attention.

It doesn't try to trap you in a loop.
It doesn't reward you for staying longer.

You come with an intention.
It responds to that intention.
Then you leave.

That's a completely different model.


## From attention capture to cognitive support

| Social media | AI tools |
|---|---|
| 👉 Interruption | 👉 Structure |
| 👉 Stimulation | 👉 Clarification |
| 👉 Emotional reaction | 👉 Execution |
| Pulls you away from your thoughts | Helps you organize them |

One fragments your thinking.
The other amplifies it.

## The bias behind the fear

We fear what is new.
We normalize what already controls us.

This is a classic cognitive bias.

We worry about the tool that is still evolving,
while ignoring the system that has already reshaped our attention, our habits, and our ability to focus.

## The real question

The question is not:

**"Is AI dangerous?"**

It is:

**"Why are we so afraid of a tool that helps us think,
and so comfortable with systems that prevent us from doing so?"**

## Final thought

The danger is not AI.

The danger is that we keep looking in the wrong direction.
